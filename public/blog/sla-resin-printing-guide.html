The Complete Guide to SLA Resin 3D Printing

March 2026 10 min read

Stereolithography — SLA — is the original 3D printing technology, and four decades after its invention it remains the gold standard for precision, surface finish, and fine detail in polymer parts. If you have ever held a 3D printed part and struggled to see any layer lines, it was almost certainly made with SLA or one of its close relatives. This guide covers everything you need to know: how the process works, how it compares to MSLA and DLP, which resin materials are available, what it does well, where it falls short, and how to get the most out of a professional SLA printing service.

How SLA Works

Stereolithography works by curing liquid photopolymer resin with ultraviolet light. The build platform sits inside a vat of liquid resin, and a UV laser traces the cross-section of each layer across the surface of the resin. Where the laser touches the liquid, it solidifies. The platform then steps down by one layer height (typically 0.025–0.1mm), fresh resin floods the surface, and the laser traces the next layer. This process repeats until the complete part is built up layer by layer.

The concept was patented by Chuck Hull — co-founder of 3D Systems — in 1984, making SLA the oldest additive manufacturing technology in commercial use. Hull's original machines were enormous, room-sized industrial systems costing hundreds of thousands of dollars. Today, professional SLA printers range from desktop units to large-format machines capable of building parts hundreds of millimeters in every dimension, while maintaining the micron-level precision that has always defined the technology.

The key attribute that distinguishes SLA from FDM is that there is no nozzle depositing material in discrete lines. The laser cures resin continuously, meaning the surface finish is limited by laser spot size (typically 0.05–0.15mm) and layer height rather than by nozzle diameter. The result is surfaces that appear smooth to the eye and feel smooth to the touch directly off the machine — a quality that FDM simply cannot match without extensive post-processing.

SLA vs. MSLA vs. DLP — Key Differences

When people say "resin printing" today, they may mean any of three related but distinct technologies: classic SLA, MSLA (Masked Stereolithography), or DLP (Digital Light Processing). Understanding the differences matters when selecting a service bureau or evaluating quote results.

SLA (Laser SLA) uses a focused UV laser beam that traces each layer point by point. Because the laser spot is small and consistent, SLA achieves the highest resolution and most uniform cure depth of the three technologies. The trade-off is speed — a laser tracing each point sequentially is inherently slower than exposing an entire layer at once. Industrial SLA machines from Formlabs, 3D Systems, and similar manufacturers produce parts with the highest surface quality and dimensional accuracy, making SLA the preferred choice for high-end prototypes, master patterns, and medical-grade components.

MSLA (Masked SLA) replaces the laser with an LCD screen used as a photomask. The screen blocks UV light selectively, exposing an entire layer simultaneously rather than tracing it point by point. MSLA is significantly faster than laser SLA and the machines are much less expensive, which is why MSLA has become the dominant technology in both desktop consumer printers (Elegoo, Anycubic) and many service bureau operations. Print quality is excellent and for most applications indistinguishable from laser SLA. The primary limitation is LCD lifespan — screens degrade over time, and a worn LCD can produce inconsistent cure across the build area.

DLP (Digital Light Processing) uses a digital projector to expose each layer. Like MSLA, it exposes the entire layer at once and is therefore fast. The difference is that DLP uses a pixel-based projector, and at larger build volumes the pixels become visible on part surfaces — the "voxel" effect. DLP is well-suited for small, high-detail parts (jewelry, dental) where the pixel size is small relative to the feature, but for larger parts the pixel artifact can be an issue. Many dental and jewelry labs use DLP specifically for its speed and accuracy at small scale.

In practical terms: if a service bureau lists "resin printing" without specifying which variant, ask. For critical cosmetic or dimensional work, laser SLA remains the benchmark. For functional prototypes and most commercial orders, MSLA is excellent and more cost-effective.

Resin Material Types

One of SLA's greatest strengths is the breadth of resin formulations available. Unlike FDM, which is largely constrained by thermoplastic filament options, resin chemistry can be tuned to achieve a wide range of mechanical and physical properties.

Standard resin is the default for most SLA jobs. It cures to a hard, smooth, slightly brittle solid. Surface finish and dimensional accuracy are excellent, making standard resin ideal for display models, master patterns for molding, and detailed visual prototypes. It is not suitable for parts that will be flexed, dropped, or subjected to repeated loading — it will crack or shatter under impact.

Tough / ABS-Like resin adds impact resistance and elongation before fracture. These resins are formulated to approximate the mechanical behavior of ABS thermoplastic — they can absorb some impact without shattering, making them suitable for enclosures, jigs and fixtures, and functional prototypes that will be handled. Ultimate strength is lower than standard resin, but toughness (energy absorbed before fracture) is much higher.

Flexible resin cures to a rubber-like solid, with Shore A hardness typically ranging from 40 to 80 depending on the formulation. Flexible resins are used for gaskets, over-mold simulations, ergonomic grips, and wearable prototypes. They tear more easily than silicone and degrade faster with UV exposure, but for short-run prototyping they are an effective stand-in for elastomeric production materials.

Castable resin is specifically formulated to burn out cleanly in a lost-wax casting process. Jewelry designers and dental technicians use castable resin to print master patterns that are then invested in plaster, burned out, and replaced with metal via casting. Castable resins must leave minimal ash residue after burnout to avoid defects in the final casting — they are highly specialized and should only be ordered from services with casting-specific process controls.

High-temperature resin is formulated for heat deflection temperatures of 238°C or more after dual-cure processing. These resins are used for mold inserts, tooling for thermoforming, duct components, and any application where a standard resin part would deform in a hot environment. They are harder and more brittle than standard resin and must be post-cured carefully to achieve their rated temperature performance.

Dental and medical-grade resin is the most regulated category, covering biocompatible formulations that are FDA-cleared for intraoral, dermal, or implant-adjacent use. Dental resins are used for surgical guides, orthodontic models, denture bases, and splints. Medical-grade resins are used for hearing aid shells, prosthetic device components, and anatomical models. These resins require ISO 10993 or equivalent biocompatibility testing and must be printed and post-cured on validated equipment by certified labs.

Industry Insight

SLA is the preferred technology for dental, hearing aid, and jewelry industries because it achieves detail levels — sub-0.1mm features, smooth surfaces, accurate undercuts — that are impossible with any extrusion-based process. A single dental lab may run dozens of SLA printers to produce hundreds of patient-specific parts per day.

What SLA Is Best For

SLA excels in applications where surface quality, fine detail, and dimensional accuracy are the primary requirements. The technology's natural strengths align closely with a specific set of industries and use cases.

Jewelry and consumer products benefit from SLA's ability to reproduce fine filigree, sharp edges, and surface textures that would be lost in FDM. Designers use SLA to produce master patterns for rubber mold making, or to print directly in castable resin for lost-wax production.

Dental and medical devices rely on SLA for patient-specific parts at scale. Orthodontic aligner models, surgical planning guides, hearing aid shells, and custom prosthetic sockets are all produced in SLA using biocompatible resins. The precision and repeatability of SLA allow dental labs to produce hundreds of unique, patient-specific parts in a single overnight build.

Tabletop miniatures and collectibles — a less industrial but enormous market — are overwhelmingly produced in MSLA. The detail achievable at 25–32mm scale with a 0.035mm XY resolution MSLA machine is extraordinary, reproducing face details, texture, and tiny decorative elements that would be impossible in any other affordable process.

Product design prototypes that need to look and feel like production parts benefit from SLA's surface finish. A consumer electronics housing, a cosmetics bottle, or a medical device casing printed in SLA with post-processing can be indistinguishable from an injection-molded part in photos and very convincing in hand.

Micro-features and precision components — including microfluidic channels, optical components, and small mechanism housings — take advantage of SLA's fine resolution and tight tolerances to produce parts that simply cannot be made any other way without expensive precision machining.

Limitations of SLA

Despite its advantages, SLA has real limitations that make it the wrong choice for many applications. Understanding them prevents expensive mistakes.

Build volume is more constrained in SLA than in FDM. Desktop MSLA machines have build volumes of roughly 130 × 80 × 160mm, and even large professional SLA systems top out around 350 × 350 × 400mm. FDM printers can be built to nearly any size, and some industrial FDM machines handle parts over a meter in length. If you need a large part, SLA may require splitting into sections.

UV sensitivity and outdoor durability are significant concerns. Photopolymer resins continue to cure under ambient UV light and will yellow, embrittle, and eventually crack if used outdoors without protection. Clear coating with UV-blocking varnish extends service life, but SLA parts are not suitable for long-term outdoor exposure in structural roles. FDM parts in ASA or PETG, or SLS nylon, are far more UV-stable.

Brittleness of standard resins limits functional use. A standard SLA part dropped from a workbench onto a concrete floor will often shatter. Tough resins mitigate this but add cost and may reduce surface quality slightly. For parts that need both cosmetic perfection and impact resistance, SLA in tough resin is a reasonable compromise — but for heavy functional use, SLS nylon or FDM in PETG will outperform.

Material cost is higher than FDM filament. Professional SLA resins typically cost $80–$250 per liter, compared to $20–$60 per kilogram for FDM filament. For large volumes of material, this cost difference is significant.

Post-processing is mandatory. SLA parts come off the printer wet with uncured resin. They must be washed in isopropyl alcohol (IPA) or a dedicated cleaning solution to remove surface resin, then post-cured in a UV chamber to achieve final mechanical properties. Skipping the post-cure leaves parts under-strength and tacky. Service bureaus include this in their process, but it adds time and operating cost.

Tolerances and Accuracy

SLA offers the best dimensional accuracy of any polymer 3D printing technology as a standard process. Typical tolerance for professional SLA services is ±0.05–0.15mm across most of the build volume, with some machines and resins achieving ±0.025mm on small features.

Several factors affect SLA accuracy in practice. Build orientation matters — features in the XY plane (parallel to the build platform) are generally more accurate than features in the Z direction, which are limited by layer height stacking. For a feature where Z-axis accuracy is critical, orient the part so that dimension falls in the XY plane.

Layer height is the primary driver of Z-axis resolution and surface smoothness. Professional SLA services typically offer 0.025mm, 0.05mm, or 0.1mm layer heights. Finer layers produce smoother surfaces and better Z resolution at the cost of longer print time. For most functional prototypes, 0.05mm is a good balance; for cosmetic masters or fine jewelry models, 0.025mm is worth the extra time.

Resin calibration — specifically the cure depth and laser power settings for a given resin — directly affects dimensional accuracy. A well-calibrated machine with fresh, temperature-controlled resin will consistently hit ±0.05mm. A machine with degraded resin, an aged LCD, or uncalibrated exposure settings may drift to ±0.2mm or worse. This is why choosing a reputable service bureau with documented quality control matters for precision work. Services like Advanced Prototyping Inc in Rochester Hills, MI, HausLab3D in Denver, CO, and Tesla Digitizing in Brooklyn, NY maintain calibration standards appropriate for professional and industrial SLA work.

For design guidelines specific to SLA — minimum wall thickness, hole compensation, overhang strategy — refer to the technology-specific rules outlined in our DFM guidelines for 3D printed parts. The key SLA-specific rules are: minimum wall 0.5mm (0.8mm preferred for freestanding features), hole compensation of +0.05–0.1mm on diameter, and tilt large flat parts 10–20 degrees off the build plate to prevent suction delamination.

Post-Processing for SLA Parts

SLA post-processing is a multi-step workflow that every professional service bureau performs as standard, but which varies in quality and thoroughness between providers. Understanding the steps helps you specify finish requirements clearly.

IPA washing removes uncured surface resin from the part. Parts are agitated in isopropyl alcohol (typically 90%+ concentration) for 5–15 minutes, either by submersion with agitation or in a dedicated wash station. Insufficient washing leaves a tacky, under-cured surface layer that compromises finish quality and mechanical properties. Some services use dedicated aqueous wash solutions as an alternative to IPA, which are less flammable and easier to dispose of.

Secondary UV curing is performed in a UV cure chamber, exposing the washed part to high-intensity UV light for 15–60 minutes depending on resin type. This final cure step hardens the part to its rated mechanical properties. Parts that skip secondary cure are softer, more flexible, and more brittle than intended. For high-temperature resins, the post-cure temperature (often combined with UV exposure in an oven) is critical to achieving the rated HDT.

Support removal is performed before or after washing depending on the service bureau's workflow. SLA supports are typically thin and snap off cleanly, but they leave witness marks (small bumps or divots) at attachment points. For cosmetic parts, support placement strategy — hiding attachment points in non-visible areas — is an important design and orientation decision.

Sanding and painting are optional finish steps. SLA parts sand easily and accept paint, primer, and clear coat well. For product presentation models or master patterns, progressive sanding from 220 to 2000 grit followed by automotive primer produces a surface that is virtually indistinguishable from injection molded ABS. Some service bureaus offer these finishing services; others deliver parts in as-printed condition and leave finishing to the customer.

How to Order SLA Printing from a Service Bureau

Getting the best results from a professional SLA service requires clear communication about your requirements before you upload a file. Here is what to specify.

File format: STL is universally accepted. STEP (ISO 10303) is preferred for precision work because it preserves exact geometry rather than a triangulated approximation, allowing the bureau to re-mesh at any resolution. For dental or medical parts, check whether the service requires a specific format for their workflow (many dental labs work in STL but some use proprietary scanners and formats).

Resin type: Specify the mechanical requirement, not just "standard resin." Tell the service bureau whether the part needs to be impact-resistant, flexible, castable, high-temperature, or biocompatible. If you have a specific resin in mind (Formlabs Tough 2000, Carbon EPX 82, etc.), request it by name. If you are unsure, describe the application and ask for a recommendation.

Layer height / resolution: For functional parts, 0.05mm is standard. For cosmetic masters or jewelry, request 0.025mm. Be aware that finer layers increase cost and lead time.

Finish level: Specify whether you need as-printed, washed and cured only, or finished (sanded, primed, painted). If support witness marks are unacceptable on certain faces, mark those faces clearly in your file or note them in the order instructions.

Tolerance requirements: If you have specific hole diameters or mating surface dimensions that are critical, call them out explicitly. A good service bureau will confirm they can hit your tolerance before committing to the job, or will advise you to add post-machining for critical dimensions.

Need cosmetic perfection

SLA is your technology. With 0.025mm layer heights, post-sanding, and priming, SLA parts are indistinguishable from injection-molded production parts in photos and feel smooth to the touch.

Need structural strength

Consider Tough Resin for moderate impact resistance, or switch to SLS nylon for isotropic structural performance. Standard SLA resin is brittle and will fracture under load or impact.
